## Core Argument (in my own words)

Rafailov et al. show that RLHF's reward-model-then-RL pipeline can be collapsed into a single supervised objective on preference pairs. The key insight is that the optimal policy under the KL-constrained reward maximization objective can be expressed directly in terms of a closed-form relationship between the reference model, the policy model, and the preference labels. This means: given (prompt, chosen, rejected) triples, you can train the model to prefer `chosen` over `rejected` without training a separate reward model or running RL. The DPO loss function is a binary cross-entropy loss on the log-ratio of the policy's probabilities for chosen vs. rejected, relative to the reference model's log-ratio.

---

## Three Design Choices I Made Based on This Paper

**1. Using the base model as its own reference**  
DPO requires a reference model to compute the log-ratio. Rafailov et al. use the SFT-initialized model as the reference. I use the base Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct model (without any SFT warm-up) as both the starting policy and the reference. This is appropriate because my training data is preference pairs, not instruction-following demonstrations — I don't have a large enough SFT dataset to warm-up first.

**2. Beta = 0.1 (conservative)**  
The beta hyperparameter controls the strength of the KL penalty against the reference model. Higher beta → policy stays closer to the reference. Rafailov et al. find that beta=0.1–0.5 works well empirically. I used 0.1 (conservative) because:
- My dataset is small (159 pairs, 60 steps) — overfitting risk is high
- The failure modes I'm training against (banned phrases, over-commitment) are specific behavioral changes, not broad style changes
- Conservative beta reduces the risk of "reward hacking" where the model learns to produce artificially low probabilities on rejected completions without actually improving behavior

**3. LoRA-only fine-tuning (not full fine-tuning)**  
Rafailov et al. use full fine-tuning. I used LoRA (rank=16) because the T4 GPU does not have enough VRAM for full DPO fine-tuning of a 1.5B model plus reference model. LoRA-DPO is well-established and the performance difference at this dataset size is negligible.

## Where I Disagree with the Paper

**Rafailov et al. claim DPO is strictly equivalent to RLHF under the KL-constrained reward objective.** This equivalence relies on assumptions that are violated in practice: (1) the preference data must be generated by the same distribution as the reference model, and (2) the preference labels must be consistent (no contradictions). My training data violates both:

1. The `rejected` responses in my training data were generated by the base model (Qwen2.5-1.5B) and by DeepSeek — two different distributions. The DPO loss treats them as if they came from the reference model's distribution, which introduces a distributional mismatch.

2. Some `rejected` responses in my dataset are near-quality outputs that fail only on one dimension (e.g., a well-grounded email that uses the word "bench" once). DPO treats all rejected completions as equally undesirable, but in reality a "bench" violation is less severe than a capacity over-commitment.

**My mitigation:** I weighted preference pairs by failure severity in the training data (see `src/training/dpo_train.py`). Pairs where `rejected` contains a BCH violation (capacity over-commitment) get 2× loss weight relative to pairs where `rejected` only contains a banned-phrase violation. This partially compensates for DPO's uniform treatment of rejected completions.

**This is a genuine unresolved limitation.** The correct solution is SimPO or ORPO (reference-free), which don't require the distributional assumption. I chose DPO for stability on T4 hardware; reference-free methods will be tested in v0.2.
